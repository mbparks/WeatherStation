# A Register for the Wind

*A short follow-up draft for Gears of Resistance / mbparks.com*

BAROGRAPH, Field Instrument No. 067, has grown a second record. Below the drum there is now an anemograph: a strip of the same chart paper, wrapped on the same cylinder, carrying seven days of wind in verdigris ink.

The Victorians had an instrument for this too, and its traces had a distinctive look. A recording anemometer's pen did not draw a smooth line; every gust flicked it upward and let it fall, so a windy day reads as a dense thicket of vertical strokes above the running trace. I kept that. Gusts on the anemograph are drawn exactly as pen flicks, and a blustery afternoon looks appropriately bristled.

Two small courtesies in the margins. The day lines on the strip fall at precisely the same positions as the day lines on the drum above, because both registers share the same cylindrical geometry; you can read a falling barometer and a rising wind in the same vertical glance, which is usually the whole story of a front. And where they land within the scale, Beaufort words are engraved along the right edge: BREEZE, FRESH, NEAR GALE, GALE, STORM. The caption below speaks Beaufort as well. The station does not merely report nine miles per hour; it reports a gentle breeze, which is what Admiral Beaufort would have wanted.
